1. The Physics of the Can

It's not an antenna, it's a Feed & Waveguide

Your copper wire is the actual antenna (a monopole). The can is a circular waveguide. It directs the energy in a single direction. However, radio waves travel differently inside a tube than they do in open air. This is the most critical concept to understand.

Inside the can, the wave bounces off the walls. This "bouncing" slows down the phase velocity of the wave. Therefore, the wavelength inside the can ($ \lambda_g $) is longer than the wavelength in free space ($ \lambda_0 $).

The "Mode" Dilemma

We want the radio wave to propagate in the dominant mode, known as TE11 (Transverse Electric 11). This mode matches the orientation of your single copper probe.

However, if the can is too wide, it allows a secondary mode called TM01. This causes interference.

  • Can too small: The wave cannot enter (Cutoff).
  • Can too big: Multiple modes (TE11 + TM01) cause signal chaos.
  • Sweet Spot: TE11 passes, TM01 is blocked.

"We need the can to filter out the bad modes and guide the good one."

2. The Governing Formulas

A. Free Space Wavelength ($\lambda_0$)

The length of the wave in open air.

$$ \lambda_0 = \frac{c}{f} $$

B. Cutoff Conditions

Selecting the correct diameter $D$.

$$ \frac{\lambda_0}{1.706} < D < \frac{\lambda_0}{1.306} $$

C. Guide Wavelength ($\lambda_g$)

The expanded wavelength inside the tube. This is the number we build with.

$$ \lambda_g = \frac{\lambda_0}{\sqrt{1 - \left( \frac{\lambda_0}{1.706 D} \right)^2}} $$

3. Construction Guide

Step 1: Can Selection & Cleaning

Based on the calculator above, select a straight-walled metal can. Coffee cans, stew cans, or paint thinner cans usually fall in the ideal range.

Myth Buster: Pringles cans (~75mm) are often too narrow, operating dangerously close to the cutoff frequency. A simple coffee can (~100mm) outperforms them significantly.

Step 2: Prepare the Copper Probe

You are using a 1.5mm pure copper wire. This acts as the radiating monopole.

1 Solder the copper wire vertically into the N-connector pin.
2 Cut the wire to the exact Probe Height (L) calculated above (measured from the brass face).
3 Trim and file the tip smooth (prevents corona discharge).

Step 3: Drilling the Can

Precision is key. The location of the hole determines the impedance match (VSWR).

  • Measure from the inside bottom face of the can.
  • Mark the distance Probe Position (S) calculated above.
  • Drill carefully using a step bit to fit your N-connector (usually 5/8").
  • Sand off the paint around the hole to ensure grounding.

Step 4: Assembly & Cabling

LMR-400 Management

LMR-400 is excellent but heavy. Do not rely on the connector to hold the weight. Zip-tie the cable to your mount.

Cable Choice:

0.5m Cable Best Signal
1.0m Cable Balanced
1.5m Cable Max Reach

Use the shortest cable (0.5m) if possible for minimal loss.
